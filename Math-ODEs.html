Preliminary concepts

The following are definitions, ideas, and other things from previous branches of mathematics and Differential Equations terminology that will be used repetitively throughout the notes:

 Def.   By order we refer to the degree of the highest derivative present in our differential equation.

 Def.   We say we have an explicit solution when we can perfectly describe the dependent variable in terms of the independent variable.

 Def.   Similarly, we have an implicit solution when we find a relation satisfying the differential equation (rather than a function).

Just as we define domains for functions, a differential equation has a domain. For a function to be a solution, it must satisfy the differential equation in all of its domain.

 Def.   For a given $n$th order differential equation, we can find its $n$-parameter family of solutions, which contain $n$ parameters.

This is similar in nature to adding $+C$ after taking the indefinite integral of a function. For $n$th order differential equations, we will have $n$ paramaters $C_i$ to manipulate our solution.

 Def.   A particular solution is one that has no arbitrary parameters to manipulate.

 Def.   Similarly, the General Solution is the $n$-parameter family that is capable of describing all the particular solutions for a given differential equation.

Visualization methods for DEs

Slope/Direction Fields (1st order DEs)

 Def.   We call Integral Curves the graph of solutions to a differential equation.

Suppose you have a first order differential equation

\[y'=F(x,y)\]

Then we can calculate slopes $y'$ at, say, integer coordinate points in the plane. With these, we can get a feel of what a solution passing through that point looks like:

\[y'=x-y\]

Finding the slope of the tangent line is a simple as plugging in the coordinates of points.

 Def.   The small "tangent" lines we draw are called line elements. A slope/direction field is a collection of line elements for a given differential equation.

Notice how, as we draw more and more line elements, we start to recognise "paths." Not surprisingly, they correspond to an integral curve. As our eyes follow the direction of tangent line segments, we see streamlines, for example:

In particular, all those streamlines (which are, of course, integral lines) come from the general solution:

$$y=x-1+\frac{C}{e^{x}} $$

Where different values of $C$ give different integra lines

Notice that the stremlines seem to never crash into each other. We formalize this with the following two definitions:

 Def.   We say a point is an ordinary point if it is part of only (and only!) one stremline/integral curve. Conversely, we say a point is a singular point if it is not a singular point (it can be part of two or more integral curves, but it can also be part of none) and it is "surrounded" by ordinary points no matter how close we see around its neighborhood.

The slope field above corresponds to the differential equation, and has for general solution:

\[y'=\frac{4(y-4)}{3x}\;\Rightarrow \; y=Cx^{4/3}+4\]

The five integral lines above correspond to $C=-2,-1,0,1,2$. Notice how all of them pass through the singular point.

1st order methods

It can't be stressed enough that, when presented with a differential equation, the first thing you should do is associate the D.E. with a given "type."

It turns out that many differential equations have no explicit solution, and although we could find the implicit solution, it has no use at all.

The best way to become a master of differential equations is to be able to identify its type, if any, and proceed with the method we know will yield a solution.

Separation of Variables

Recall that first order differential equations can be written in terms of a function $F$ as:

\[y'=F(x,y)\]

Now, consider differential equations with the form (and its expansion):

\begin{align*} A(x,y)y' +B(x,y)&= 0 \\ A(x,y)\frac{dy}{dx} +B(x,y)&= 0 \\ dx \left( A(x,y)\frac{dy}{dx} +B(x,y) \right) &= dx\cdot 0 \\ A(x,y)dy +B(x,y)dx &= 0 \end{align*}

If you find a way to to rewrite the last expression in the form:

\[f(x)dx+g(y)dy=0\]

Then we are good! Notice that our variables are isolated from each other. They are separated. A solution is found by integrating everything:
